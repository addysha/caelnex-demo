Critical 01 /04 Launch ops · Flight SW

The valve that nobody
told launch ops about

T-minus 18 hours. A design change made three weeks ago quietly changed the world.

The scene

It's 2 a.m. in Mahia. The launch director is stepping through the final readiness review when a technician flags something odd in the propulsion checklist — a valve torque specification that doesn't match the unit physically installed on the vehicle. Engineering in Long Beach changed the spec 22 days ago. The change was logged in CATIA, approved in Confluence, and completely invisible to the launch ops team who built their checklist off a snapshot from six weeks prior.

This isn't hypothetical. In aerospace, configuration drift between design systems and operational documents is endemic. Nobody is malicious. The systems just don't talk. The question is whether you find it at T-18 hours or T-4 seconds.

How Nexus resolves this

Nexus maintains a live configuration graph where the valve is a node with edges to its spec document, the checklist line item, the test record, and the part serial number on the vehicle. When the engineer in Long Beach commits the spec change, Nexus immediately flags every downstream document that referenced the old value — including the launch checklist. The launch director gets a notification within minutes, not weeks.

High 02 /04 Engineering · Supply chain

The supplier who shipped
the wrong revision

On paper, every part is green. In the real world, one isn't.

The scene

A batch of 47 composite brackets arrives from a supplier in Germany. They're logged into the system, QC stamps the paperwork, and they enter the vehicle build flow. Two weeks into integration, a sharp-eyed technician notices the hole pattern is 2mm off. The supplier shipped Revision C. The vehicle requires Revision D. Revision D was issued four months ago. Nobody sent the supplier the updated drawing because the drawing lived in a PDM system the supplier doesn't have access to, and the email with the update went to a contact who left the company.

Two weeks of integration work is suspect. The launch window is in six weeks. The supplier lead time is four. You're now in a room doing math that doesn't add up, with a customer who paid for a specific launch date.

How Nexus resolves this

Nexus tracks part genealogy end-to-end: every component has a lineage node that includes the revision it was ordered against, the revision current at time of shipment, and the revision required by the vehicle configuration. When a new drawing revision is issued, Nexus automatically flags all open purchase orders for that part and notifies the supplier contact — with the new drawing attached. Incoming parts are scanned against the expected revision at receiving.

Medium 03 /04 Operations · Compliance

The audit that ate
three engineers for a month

The DoD wants a full trail. The trail lives in fourteen different places.

The scene

A DCSA auditor requests evidence of ITAR compliance for a defense payload mission — specifically, who accessed what controlled technical data, when, from where, and whether they were authorised. The compliance manager opens her laptop and begins what she privately calls "the archaeology project." Confluence logs. Git history. Email threads. A shared drive folder that three people have modified with no version history. The SharePoint that one business unit still uses. She will spend the next 19 working days reconstructing a chain of custody that should have been automatic.

This isn't just waste. A failed or incomplete ITAR audit carries criminal exposure for individuals, not just fines for the company. And Rocket Lab is doing more defense work every year.

How Nexus resolves this

Every action in Nexus writes an immutable, timestamped audit event: who viewed a document, who approved a change, what their authorisation level was, and what network/location they accessed from. Controlled technical data is tagged at creation. Access is gated by role and citizenship. An ITAR audit package for a specific mission is a single export: structured, formatted, ready to hand to the auditor. What took 19 days takes 40 minutes.

Monitor 04 /04 Engineering · Leadership

The engineer who
remembered everything

He retired on a Friday. By Monday, nobody knew why the decision was made.

The scene

Marcus has been at Rocket Lab for seven years. He was there for the early Electron failures. He knows exactly why Stage 2's avionics bay has that unusual thermal blanket configuration — it was a field fix after an anomaly on Flight 4, documented in a Word doc on his personal laptop and never formally logged because the launch was the next morning and the fix worked. Marcus is 58. He accepts an offer in January. His last day is quiet. A farewell cake, a kind email from Peter Beck. Six months later, a thermal anomaly on Flight 39 has the avionics team puzzled. Someone says: "Marcus would have known."

This is the slowest-moving, most underestimated risk in aerospace. Every year of institutional knowledge that leaves through the door is a risk that doesn't show up until it absolutely does.

How Nexus resolves this

Nexus builds a decision graph alongside the configuration graph. Every anomaly disposition, every field change, every design decision that was contentious — engineers are prompted to link it to the component it affected with a brief rationale note. Over time this becomes an institutional memory layer: when an anomaly recurs, Nexus surfaces all prior decisions made on that component or subsystem, who made them, and why. The AI layer adds cross-mission pattern matching — "this thermal signature appeared on Flights 4, 11, and 39. Here are the dispositions from each."
